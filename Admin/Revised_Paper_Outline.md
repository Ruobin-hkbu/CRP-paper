# Revised Paper Outline

## Working Title
**The Poem Before the Poet: AI Co-Creation, Administered Style, and the Politics of Resistance in Poetry Writing**

## Central Claim
This paper argues that AI poetry co-creation does not simply assist writing but structures the field of stylistic choice in advance, making some formal options recurrent and others difficult to elicit. Drawing on Marcuse and Adorno, then bridging to Haraway through Feenberg, the paper shows that writers' resistance emerges not from interaction alone but from moments when they recognize and contest AI-administered defaults. The paper makes bounded claims based on a specific dataset rather than universal claims about everything AI can or cannot produce.

## Revised Research Questions
1. What may be the effect of AI poetry co-creation on writers' stylistic selections, especially choices not represented in model suggestions?
2. Under what interaction conditions do students remain within AI-administered defaults versus explicitly resisting them?
3. Which stylistic features in this study were repeatedly suggested, rarely suggested, or consistently difficult to elicit from the model?

## Full Outline

### I. Introduction
1. Introduce the problem of AI poetry as a form of co-creation that appears open-ended but is already shaped by model preselection.
2. Define the paper's main intervention: the issue is not only output quality, but how AI pre-curates style before human judgment begins.
3. Present the study's bounded scope: this paper does not claim to prove what AI cannot generate in principle, only what was difficult to elicit in this dataset.
4. State the three revised research questions.
5. Present the core argument: both exemplar-led and negotiation-oriented turns are structurally pre-critical, but negotiation can generate awareness of administered defaults and thereby open the possibility of resistance.

### II. Literature and Problem Context
1. Situate the study in scholarship on AI-generated poetry and human-AI creative collaboration.
2. Summarize the dominant optimistic account of co-creation: efficiency, inspiration, confidence, and accessibility.
3. Identify the gap in that literature: most studies focus on usefulness, output quality, or satisfaction, not on how AI pre-filters stylistic possibility.
4. Introduce the paper's specific contribution: a critical account of style selection, administered aesthetics, and resistance in human-AI poetry writing.

### III. Theoretical Framework
#### III.1 Marcuse: One-Dimensionality, Language, and the Foreclosure of Alternatives
1. Explain one-dimensionality as a narrowing of thinkable and sayable possibilities.
2. Emphasize that for Marcuse, forms of discourse organize what appears available to thought and response.
3. Connect this to AI poetry: model suggestions appear helpful and natural while making excluded alternatives invisible.
4. Establish one analytical criterion for the paper: moments when writers seek, invent, or demand new forms of language that break from model defaults.

#### III.2 Adorno: Administered Aesthetics and the Logic of Standardization
1. Explain Adorno's account of standardization and administered culture.
2. Clarify intentionality: AI itself does not intend, but institutions, datasets, platform design, and optimization goals do organize the conditions of output.
3. Frame the paper's key diagnostic question: does AI enact administration directly, or does it produce a facsimile of administration through probabilistic style selection?
4. Connect administered aesthetics to personalized interaction, where control appears as collaboration.

#### III.3 Feenberg as Conceptual Bridge
1. Introduce Feenberg to connect critical diagnosis with technological redesign and democratization.
2. Show why Feenberg resolves the bridge problem between Marcuse/Adorno and Haraway.
3. Argue that critique alone is insufficient; the question is how interface design and interaction structure can enable users to recognize and resist defaults.

#### III.4 Haraway and Critical-Relational Co-Creation
1. Present Haraway as a framework for situated, relational engagement with the machine.
2. Recast AI not as neutral tool or autonomous creator, but as a technical other whose limits and tendencies must be engaged critically.
3. Use Haraway to define the paper's normative horizon: not pure rejection of AI, but more reflexive and resistant forms of co-creation.

### IV. Method and Corpus
1. Describe the dataset and study boundary clearly: number of participants, platform setting, and type of writing sessions.
2. Explain why the paper makes conditional claims tied to this corpus.
3. Define the interaction categories used in the analysis, such as exemplar-led turns, negotiation-oriented turns, and explicitly resistant turns if supported by the data.
4. Explain the coding strategy for stylistic features.

#### IV.1 Operationalizing "Difficult to Elicit"
1. Replace absolute claims about what AI cannot generate with a bounded empirical definition.
2. Define a difficult-to-elicit feature as one that remains absent or only partial after repeated prompt variation and at least two explicit revision attempts.
3. List the style features to be tracked, such as syntax compression, semantic opacity, anti-lyric fragmentation, dialect forms, metrical irregularity, or other features actually present in your corpus.
4. Explain the coding rubric: present, partially present, absent.
5. Clarify how participant revisions will be compared with AI suggestions to identify what humans added that the model did not supply.

### V. Findings I: How AI Structures Stylistic Choice
1. Show which features or stylistic tendencies the AI repeatedly suggests across the sample.
2. Identify which formal tendencies appear as defaults, such as smoothing, coherence, recognizable lyric diction, or corpus-typical figurative language.
3. Demonstrate how these defaults shape the writer's perceived option space before explicit evaluation begins.
4. Keep claims tied to patterns in the sample rather than broader generalizations.

### VI. Findings II: Negotiation, Friction, and the Limits of Apparent Agency
1. Compare exemplar-led turns with negotiation-oriented turns.
2. Argue that both remain pre-critical in structure because the AI has already curated the option set.
3. Show why negotiation can still matter: friction helps writers notice recurrence, genericity, or missing forms.
4. Use evidence from the dataset to identify when negotiation stays within administered defaults and when it begins to produce explicit recognition of those defaults.

### VII. Findings III: Resistance and the Emergence of Critical Awareness
1. Identify cases where writers explicitly rejected recurring AI tendencies or named what the model kept avoiding.
2. Analyze moments when participants introduced stylistic features absent from the model's suggestions.
3. Argue that resistance is not just editing output but recognizing the selection logic behind the output.
4. Connect these moments back to Marcuse's new forms of language, Adorno's critique of administration, and Haraway's critical-relational practice.

### VIII. Discussion
1. Synthesize the findings into the paper's main argument: AI co-creation shapes style not only by generating text but by organizing the field of available options.
2. Clarify the theoretical payoff: Marcuse explains foreclosure, Adorno explains administration, Feenberg explains the possibility of redesign, and Haraway explains situated critical relation.
3. Address the distinction between administration and facsimile more carefully: even if AI does not intend, its infrastructural organization can still have administrating effects.
4. Emphasize the paper's bounded claim: the study suggests recurrent tendencies in this sample, not universal truths about all models or all poetry.

### IX. Implications
#### IX.1 Pedagogical Implications
1. Teachers should not treat AI negotiation alone as evidence of critical autonomy.
2. Instruction should guide students to identify recurrent defaults, absent features, and their own reasons for accepting or rejecting suggestions.

#### IX.2 Design Implications
1. Interfaces should make stylistic variation and model tendencies more visible.
2. Systems should support comparison, refusal, and reflective commentary rather than only fluent output generation.

#### IX.3 Research Implications
1. Larger datasets and comparative model studies would be needed to support stronger claims.
2. Future work could test whether platform design changes make resistance more likely.

### X. Conclusion
1. Reiterate that the paper is not about proving AI's total incapacity, but about showing how AI co-creation may normalize some forms while making others difficult to reach.
2. Restate the main conclusion: critical engagement begins when writers move from selecting within AI-administered options to recognizing and contesting the logic that organizes those options.
3. End with a calibrated claim about what this study indicates for AI writing, literary pedagogy, and the politics of style.
