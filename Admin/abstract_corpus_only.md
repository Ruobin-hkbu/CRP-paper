This paper examines human-AI poetry co-creation through a corpus-centered analysis of classroom writing sessions, asking how stylistic options are shaped in interaction and when writers begin to resist model defaults. Rather than treating technical settings as causal variables, the study focuses on turn-level evidence from a purpose-built platform used in a university creative writing seminar for advanced L2 English learners. The dataset combines chat transcripts, interaction metadata, and reflective discussion records, enabling analysis of both textual outcomes and collaborative process.

Methodologically, the paper uses a bounded mixed approach: quantitative distributional coding of interaction functions and stylistic features, combined with qualitative close reading of representative session trajectories. AI turns are coded as repairing/fixing, exemplar giving, or creative divergence; human turns are coded as accept, modify, reject, or redirect. To avoid overclaiming, the paper operationalizes difficult to elicit as a corpus-level category based on repeated prompting and revision attempts, not as proof of absolute machine incapacity.

Findings are expected to show that AI suggestions recurrently foreground particular stylistic defaults while making other formal possibilities less available without sustained user pressure. Critical agency, in turn, depends less on interaction volume than on whether participants explicitly identify and contest recurring patterns. The study offers a transparent framework for linking critical theory to observable co-creative practice through evidence-calibrated claims.
