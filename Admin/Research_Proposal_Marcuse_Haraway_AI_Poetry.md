# Article Proposal

## The Poem Before the Poet: Pre-Critical AI Curation and the Politics of Style in Human-AI Writing

## 1. Aim and Scope
This article analyzes how AI systems pre-filter stylistic options in human-AI poetry co-creation, examining the standardization and administered aesthetics embedded in algorithmic style selection. Data from 30 participants writing poetry with a customized AI chatbot reveal a critical pattern: AI suggestions—trained on corpus-dominant contemporary poetry—present pre-filtered stylistic choices that writers evaluate without conscious awareness of what alternatives the training process has systematically suppressed. The research distinguishes two modes of interaction: exemplar-led turns (ready-to-use, polished outputs) and negotiation-oriented turns (partial suggestions requiring rework). Both modes are structurally pre-critical: in each case the AI has already processed, filtered, and presented stylistic decisions before the writer responds. Exemplar-led turns make this transparent—the output is ready to accept. Negotiation-oriented turns create the appearance of critical judgment, but the writer is still working within options the AI has already shaped. However, the friction of negotiation may develop writers' awareness of those pre-determined defaults, and that awareness can become the condition for genuine critical engagement. The article argues that both modes instantiate one-dimensional thought (Marcuse) and the administered logic of the culture industry (Adorno), but that negotiation-oriented turns carry a developmental potential that exemplar-led turns foreclose.

## 2. Research Questions
1. What styles does the AI chatbot's training data systematically privilege, marginalize, or exclude, and how does this pre-determination constrain the writer's perceived range of stylistic choice across both exemplar-led and negotiation-oriented interaction modes?
2. In what ways do negotiation-oriented turns remain pre-critical despite appearing to involve active judgment, and under what conditions can the friction of negotiation develop into genuine awareness of administered defaults?
3. What interaction conditions and design principles can move writers from pre-critical engagement—whether passive acceptance or active negotiation—toward critical recognition of the AI's systematic stylistic tendencies?

## 3. Primary Theoretical Framework
### 3.1 Marcuse: One-Dimensionality and the Foreclosure of Alternatives
Marcuse's critique of one-dimensional thought explains how technological systems present a pre-organized field of options that forecloses awareness of alternatives. In AI poetry, the model's suggested forms appear as natural, available, and comprehensible—while unmade alternatives remain invisible. Writers evaluate within the pre-selected set rather than questioning the selection process itself. This narrowing is not felt as constraint: the suggestions are often competent, helpful, and aesthetically defensible. Yet they enact a systematic reduction of possibility space before conscious judgment begins.

### 3.2 Adorno: Administered Aesthetics and the Culture Industry Logic
Adorno's analysis of the culture industry—which replaces genuine aesthetic experience with administered, standardized consumption—provides the article's diagnostic framework. In Adorno's account, the culture industry forecloses autonomous judgment by presenting pre-packaged, optimized forms as natural and inevitable. Aesthetic experience is supplanted by passive reception of what the industry determines to be acceptable.

AI poetry systems extend this logic into creative collaboration itself. The model's suggestions are not neutral facilitators but "administered" style: outputs optimized for corpus-acceptability, trained to avoid jarring divergence from statistical norms, and smoothly integrated into familiar genre conventions. This is not brutally imposed but seductively offered—the user experiences the suggestions as helpful, viable, natural. Yet at the formal level, the AI systematically normalizes particular rhythmic patterns, vocabulary choices, and figurative conventions while rendering invisible the alternatives that fall outside its training distribution.

Crucially, Adorno's critique applies here with special force because the culture industry's standardization is now personalized. Where mid-century advertising targeting was mass-level, algorithmic curation operates at the level of individual interaction. Each suggestion feels tailored, responsive, even collaborative—while actually instantiating the same logic of administered aesthetics. The writer experiences choice while systematically encountering only those forms the algorithm has pre-selected as high-probability. One-dimensionality becomes subjectively invisible because it is mediated through the appearance of dialogue.

## 4. Supplementary Theoretical and Empirical Support
Meyer (2023) provides quantitative evidence for administered aesthetics: LLM outputs systematically converge on corpus-dominant stylistic patterns, demonstrable at the level of lexical choice, syntax, and phonemic distribution. This empirically grounds Adorno's diagnosis of standardization. Haraway's kin-making offers a counter-practice: recognizing the machine's difference not as tool to optimize but as alterity to engage critically. Clark et al. (2018) documents human-in-the-loop negotiation as a space where agency can re-emerge precisely through friction, partial understanding, and revision-cycles that disrupt smooth automation. Barthes's distinction between style (singular, idiosyncratic) and writing (normalized, ideological) frames the stakes: does AI co-creation amplify writing at the expense of style?

## 5. Fundamental Argument Anchor: Resistance to Administered Defaults Across Three Interaction Modes
The study data map onto a spectrum of resistance to administered aesthetics:

1. **Uncritical acceptance (Instrumental):** The writer selects or lightly edits AI-curated outputs without interrogating the frame. Marcuse explains the risk: the pre-selected range of options becomes invisible as a constraint. Adorno diagnoses this as aesthetic administration mistaken for choice—the writer experiences freedom while systematically encountering only high-probability forms.

2. **Negotiation within administered options (Dialogic):** The writer revises suggestions, pushes back on specific lines, and demands alternatives. This appears to be critical judgment, but it is still pre-critical in Marcuse's sense: the AI has already decided which styles to produce before the writer responds, and revision operates within that pre-shaped range. The writer is exercising choice, but the available options have already been filtered. Nevertheless, this mode has developmental significance. Because the writer must repeatedly engage with specific suggestions rather than accept them wholesale, she is more likely to notice what the model consistently produces, what it cannot produce, and where its outputs feel inert or generic. That noticing—still pre-critical—can accumulate into the awareness necessary for transitioning to mode 3.

3. **Interrogation of administered form (Critical-relational):** The writer identifies what styles the AI systematically produces and which alternatives it systematically cannot generate. She questions whether administered forms serve her aesthetic intent. She asserts that the constraint is not natural or inevitable but imposed by training data and architectural choices. This is the site of genuine resistance—addressing not individual outputs but the logic of administered aesthetics itself.

Empirically, both exemplar-led and negotiation-oriented turns are pre-critical in structure—both place the writer inside a range of options the AI has already determined. The difference is developmental: exemplar-led turns offer no friction that might make the pre-determination visible, while negotiation-oriented turns, through repeated encounter and revision, can generate the awareness that administered defaults are patterns, not inevitabilities. Writers who move from negotiation to explicit recognition of the AI's systematic tendencies—naming what the model cannot produce, identifying what it always produces—are those who develop genuine critical distance and report higher ownership and creative satisfaction.

## 6. Implications and Conclusion
If AI poetry systems instantiate administered aesthetics directly within collaborative writing, then implications follow for interface design, pedagogy, and evaluation. Writers need explicit visibility into how suggestions are generated and filtered so they can recognize recurrent defaults instead of treating outputs as neutral proposals. Systems should also preserve productive friction by presenting alternatives that differ in rhythm, diction, and figurative structure, rather than repeatedly smoothing toward high-probability forms. Prompting should not only request revision but also require explanation of judgment, asking writers to state why they keep, reject, or transform a suggestion and what aesthetic criteria they are applying.

The pedagogical consequence is that negotiation with AI should not be treated as evidence of critical autonomy by itself. Negotiation often remains pre-critical because the model has already selected the range of available options. However, repeated negotiation can still be valuable when instruction is designed to turn local revision into pattern recognition. When writers are guided to identify what the model repeatedly produces, what it avoids, and what it cannot generate, they move from reactive editing toward explicit critique of the underlying selection process. This is the transition from managed participation to autonomous aesthetic judgment.

The central conclusion is that AI-assisted poetry writing should be evaluated by the quality of human critical engagement, not by fluency, speed, or apparent collaboration. Both exemplar-led and negotiation-oriented turns begin inside pre-determined output spaces, but only some interactions help writers recognize that condition and act against it. A human-centered practice therefore requires more than better prompts or better model output. It requires sustained methods that expose standardization, support refusal of default forms, and expand the writer's capacity to define aesthetic direction on her own terms.
